# Knowledge — GTM Strategy

Covers: Segmentation (STP), Market Sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM).

**Segmentation (STP: Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning)**

Market segmentation is the practice of dividing a heterogeneous market into groups of customers (segments) who share characteristics relevant to how they will respond to a product or message, on the premise that a single undifferentiated offer or message serves a diverse market poorly. The concept is generally attributed to Wendell Smith (1956) and was developed further within the broader STP framework (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning), a sequence popularised through Philip Kotler's marketing management literature: first divide the market into segments, then select (target) which segment(s) to prioritise, then define positioning, how the product should be understood relative to alternatives in the mind of the targeted segment.

Segmentation can be built on several types of variable, and most practical segmentations combine more than one. Demographic/firmographic variables (age, income, company size, industry) are easiest to obtain but weakest at predicting behaviour on their own. Behavioural variables (usage patterns, purchase history, feature usage) are more predictive but require existing usage data, making them harder to apply to prospective, not-yet-acquired customers. Needs-based or jobs-based segmentation (grouping by the underlying job or need being solved, see [[discovery]]) is generally considered the most actionable for product and positioning decisions, because it groups customers by what actually drives their choice rather than by an attribute merely correlated with it. Positioning work associated with Al Ries and Jack Trout emphasises that positioning is a claim made in the customer's mind relative to competitive alternatives, not a description of the product's attributes in isolation; a positioning statement is only meaningful with reference to what it is being positioned against.

A well-formed segment is usually described against a small number of criteria: it should be measurable (its size and characteristics can be established), substantial (large enough to be worth a distinct go-to-market motion), accessible (reachable through some channel at reasonable cost), and differentiable (it responds meaningfully differently to the offer than other segments; if two "segments" behave identically, they are not usefully separate segments).

Known limitations: segmentation built solely on demographic or firmographic variables frequently fails to predict actual buying or usage behaviour, since two firms of identical size and industry can have entirely different underlying needs. Segments can also become stale as a market or product matures, particularly if defined around a product's current customer base rather than around the broader addressable need, which can cause a company to keep serving its known segment well while missing an adjacent one better suited to a newer product capability.

Connections: needs-based segmentation is essentially JTBD (see [[discovery]]) applied to grouping the market rather than to a single interview; a segmentation exercise that ignores the underlying job is vulnerable to the same critique JTBD makes of persona-based user stories, that surface attributes predict behaviour poorly compared to circumstance and desired progress. Positioning, the third stage of STP, is also the natural link to Geoffrey Moore's crossing-the-chasm framing referenced elsewhere in this repo's project instructions: different segments (early market vs mainstream) generally require materially different positioning, not the same message delivered more broadly.

**Market Sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM)**

TAM/SAM/SOM is a nested framework for estimating market size at three levels of specificity. TAM (Total Addressable Market) is the total revenue opportunity if the product captured 100% of demand for the category it serves, globally or in whatever total scope is relevant, with no constraint from the company's actual go-to-market reach. SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) is the subset of TAM that the company could realistically serve given its actual product, geography, regulatory constraints, and business model (e.g., a company selling only in English-speaking markets has a SAM smaller than the global TAM for the same product category). SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) is the subset of SAM the company could realistically capture over a defined near-term period, given competitive dynamics, sales/distribution capacity, and pricing.

The core mechanic for estimation is typically one of two approaches, often triangulated together. Top-down sizing starts from a broad, often third-party, industry statistic (total category spend, number of potential customer organisations) and narrows it by applying successive filters that reflect the company's actual addressable and obtainable scope. Bottom-up sizing starts from unit economics the company can directly evidence (average revenue per customer, actual or piloted conversion rates, achievable customer count given known channels and sales capacity) and multiplies upward to a market estimate; bottom-up sizing is generally regarded as more credible for an early-stage or unlaunched product, since top-down industry statistics are easy to cite but usually only loosely related to what the company can actually capture.

Known limitations: top-down-only sizing is a common source of unrealistic market claims, since selecting a large, defensible-sounding industry total and asserting a plausible-sounding percentage share produces a SOM figure with no real evidentiary link to the company's actual capacity to capture it. TAM figures are also frequently stated without a clearly defined boundary (unclear whether the category includes adjacent use cases or only the narrow current use case), making TAM figures from different sources difficult to compare. Market sizing additionally tends to understate the effect of market creation or expansion: for a genuinely novel category, current TAM as measured reflects the old category the new product is replacing or expanding, not the market that will exist once the new product changes customer behaviour, so a strictly historical TAM figure can materially understate the true addressable opportunity for a category-creating product (and, conversely, overstate it if the category fails to form as assumed).

Connections: market sizing is normally scoped to a specific target segment identified through STP (above), not to an undifferentiated total market, since SAM and SOM are only meaningful relative to a defined, reachable customer group and go-to-market motion. It is also a natural viability-risk check in Cagan's framing: an initiative that scores well on discovery evidence of value and usability can still fail on viability if the addressable and obtainable market, once rigorously bottom-up sized, is too small to justify the investment.
