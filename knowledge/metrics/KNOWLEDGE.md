# Knowledge — Metrics

Covers: HEART framework, AARRR (Pirate Metrics).

**HEART**

HEART is a framework for structuring user experience and product metrics, developed by Kerry Rodden and colleagues at Google (published 2010) to address the difficulty of measuring UX quality with metrics that are both meaningful and practically instrumentable. HEART is an acronym for five categories: Happiness (subjective satisfaction, typically measured via surveys such as satisfaction scores or Net Promoter Score), Engagement (depth and frequency of user interaction with the product, e.g., sessions per user per week, or a defined set of core actions taken), Adoption (rate at which new users or new features are taken up, e.g., percentage of eligible users who used a feature within a defined window), Retention (rate at which users return or remain active over time, e.g., cohort-based return rate at defined intervals), and Task success (traditional usability metrics for a specific task, e.g., completion rate, time on task, error rate).

The framework is paired with the Goals-Signals-Metrics (GSM) process for operationalising it: for each HEART category relevant to the product or feature under evaluation, the team first states a Goal in plain language, then identifies Signals that would indicate progress toward that goal (observable user behaviours or stated attitudes), then selects specific Metrics that quantify those signals in a way that is actually instrumentable in the product's analytics. This ordering (goal before signal before metric) exists specifically to avoid the common failure of picking a metric because it is easy to measure, then retrofitting a goal to justify it.

Not every category applies to every product or feature; the original guidance is to select the subset of the five categories genuinely relevant to the thing being measured rather than mechanically filling in all five for every evaluation, since forcing an irrelevant category produces a weak or misleading metric.

Known limitations: Happiness metrics (surveys, NPS) are subject to response bias and typically have low response rates, and translate slowly into action since they are lagging and aggregate; Task success metrics are well suited to a specific, scoped task flow (e.g., checkout) but do not by themselves capture whether a product is valuable over time, only whether a given flow is usable. Because HEART is a categorisation and process framework rather than a specific set of metrics, its output quality depends entirely on the rigor of the GSM exercise; a poorly run GSM produces plausible-looking metrics with a weak connection to the stated goal.

Connections: HEART is a general-purpose UX/product metrics framework applicable both to steady-state feature evaluation and to defining input metrics that feed a North Star Metric's driver tree (see [[goal-setting]]); Engagement, Adoption, and Retention categories in particular are the categories most likely to map directly onto North Star driver metrics. It complements AARRR by being feature- and experience-centred rather than funnel-centred; a team evaluating a specific feature is usually better served by HEART, while a team diagnosing where in the customer lifecycle users are lost is usually better served by AARRR.

**AARRR (Pirate Metrics)**

AARRR, commonly called "Pirate Metrics" for the way the acronym is pronounced, is a framework for structuring growth and lifecycle metrics around five stages of the customer journey, introduced by Dave McClure in 2007. The five stages are Acquisition (how users find and first arrive at the product, e.g., channel-level signup or visit counts), Activation (whether a new user reaches a first meaningful experience of value, often operationalised as a defined "aha moment" or first-value event), Retention (whether users come back and remain engaged over time, typically measured via cohort return curves), Referral (whether existing users bring in new users, e.g., invites sent, viral coefficient), and Revenue (whether the product converts user activity into monetary value, e.g., conversion to paid, average revenue per user, lifetime value).

The core mechanic is funnel diagnosis: because the five stages are ordered as a sequential journey, plotting conversion or drop-off at each stage identifies where in the lifecycle the product is losing the most users, which focuses improvement effort on the stage with the largest leverage rather than spreading attention evenly across the whole journey. Activation is frequently the stage requiring the most product-specific definition work, since "reaching a meaningful first experience of value" is not a generic event and must be defined per product (typically through a combination of usage-pattern analysis, correlating early actions with long-term retention, and qualitative validation).

Known limitations: the framework's linear five-stage structure fits a single-user, transactional SaaS or consumer product reasonably well but fits more complex models awkwardly, particularly multi-sided marketplaces (where supply-side and demand-side users may need separate AARRR funnels), enterprise sales-led motions (where "acquisition" and "revenue" are mediated by a sales cycle rather than self-serve conversion), and product-led growth motions with strong bottom-up referral loops that do not map cleanly onto a single linear stage. Treating the five stages as strictly sequential can also obscure the fact that retention and referral behaviours can begin before "activation" is formally reached, or that revenue can precede full activation in some pricing models (e.g., paid trials).

Connections: AARRR and the activation funnel are the natural home for adoption-funnel work described under Cagan's viability/adoption concerns and under behaviour-change models generally; Activation in particular is the stage most directly informed by JTBD (see [[discovery]]), since a well-defined "aha moment" is usually the point at which a user first experiences progress against their job. AARRR's Retention stage and HEART's Retention category measure overlapping territory but from different angles: AARRR treats retention as one stage in a broader acquisition-to-revenue funnel, while HEART treats it as one of several parallel lenses on a specific feature or product experience.
