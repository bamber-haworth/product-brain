# Rules — Goal Setting

Confirmed patterns to apply by default. Demote to `HYPOTHESES.md` if later contradicted.

1. Write Key Results as customer or business outcomes (behaviour change, adoption, retention, revenue), never as delivery milestones ("ship X"). A Key Result phrased as a shipped feature is a disguised roadmap item, not evidence that value was achieved.

2. Keep each Objective to two to five Key Results. More dilutes focus and signals the objective itself was insufficiently scoped.

3. Do not tie OKR attainment scores directly to individual performance ratings or compensation. Doing so incentivises sandbagging ambitious objectives down to safely achievable ones, which defeats the purpose of setting stretch goals.

4. Treat a quarterly OKR miss scored around 0.6–0.7 as an acceptable, even expected, outcome for a genuinely ambitious objective. Consistently scoring 1.0 is a signal the objective was set too conservatively, not a signal of good performance.

5. Select a single North Star Metric that reflects value the customer actually receives, not company activity (e.g., not signups). Pair it with at least one guardrail metric to catch optimisation that grows the North Star Metric while harming the business (Goodhart's law risk).

6. Before committing to an initiative on the roadmap, check whether it plausibly moves the North Star Metric or one of its named driver metrics. An initiative that scores well on RICE or MoSCoW but has no credible link to the North Star Metric is a candidate for deprioritisation.

7. For multi-sided marketplaces or multi-product portfolios, do not force a single company-wide North Star Metric if it clearly fails to represent value across distinct segments or product lines. Use one North Star Metric per major segment or line instead.
