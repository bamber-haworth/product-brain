# Knowledge — Prioritisation

Covers: RICE, MoSCoW, Kano Model.

**RICE**

RICE is a scoring model for prioritising a backlog of candidate initiatives, developed at Intercom and popularised by Sean McBride. Each initiative is scored on four factors, multiplied together (with one factor divided) to produce a single comparable number: Reach (how many customers or users the initiative will affect within a defined time period, expressed as a count), Impact (the size of effect on each affected user, typically scored on a coarse scale such as 3 = massive, 2 = high, 1 = medium, 0.5 = low, 0.25 = minimal), Confidence (how much evidence supports the reach and impact estimates, expressed as a percentage, e.g. 100%, 80%, 50%), and Effort (the total person-time required, expressed in person-months). The formula is (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort. Higher scores indicate higher priority.

The Confidence factor exists specifically to discourage inflated Impact or Reach estimates made without evidence: an initiative whose impact is guessed rather than evidenced should carry a lower confidence multiplier, pulling its score down proportionately. RICE is a relative comparison tool for ranking initiatives against each other within one backlog; the absolute score has no independent meaning and is not comparable across teams or time periods using different estimation conventions.

Known limitations: RICE produces a single number from what are in practice highly uncertain estimates, particularly Reach and Impact for anything not yet built, which can create false precision and end debate prematurely. It has no mechanism for strategic fit or sequencing dependencies (an initiative might score low on RICE alone but be a necessary precursor to a higher-scoring one). It also does not account for risk type; a high-RICE initiative that is high risk on feasibility grounds is scored the same as one that is high risk on value grounds, even though the appropriate response (a feasibility spike vs a customer test) differs.

Connections: RICE operates entirely in the solution space, ranking already-identified candidate solutions; it presumes the value risk question ("is this worth building at all," per Cagan) has already been answered by discovery, and is not a substitute for that discovery. It pairs naturally with Torres's assumption mapping (see [[discovery]]): a low-Confidence RICE score is a direct signal that assumption testing is needed before the initiative should be trusted at its stated score.

**MoSCoW**

MoSCoW is a prioritisation technique that sorts requirements or features into four categories: Must have (non-negotiable for this release; without it, the release fails or is unusable), Should have (important but not release-blocking; painful to omit but workable), Could have (desirable, included only if time and resource allow), and Won't have (explicitly out of scope for this timeframe, stated to close off debate rather than left ambiguous). It originated with Dai Clegg and is closely associated with the DSDM (Dynamic Systems Development Method) agile delivery framework, where it is used to negotiate scope for a fixed-time, fixed-resource delivery window.

The core mechanic is a categorical sort rather than a numerical score, intended to be fast, discussable in a stakeholder workshop, and legible to non-specialists. Its primary use case is scope negotiation for a fixed deadline (e.g., a contractual release date or a fixed-length sprint), where the question is "what is the minimum that must ship," not "which of these has the best return." A commonly cited discipline is to timebox the "Must have" category to a strict minority of total effort (a frequently cited convention is roughly 60% of effort as an upper bound on Musts), on the grounds that if everything is a Must, the categorisation has failed to do its job of creating tradeoffs.

Known limitations: MoSCoW provides no method for deciding which of several candidate features is more valuable; it only sorts already-agreed-necessary items into buckets. Because status is often assigned by stakeholder assertion rather than by evidence, it is vulnerable to category inflation, where every stakeholder insists their item is a Must, collapsing the technique's usefulness. It also does not naturally accommodate outcome framing; a Must-have is usually stated as a feature or requirement, not as a customer outcome, which can pull a team back toward output thinking if used carelessly.

Connections: MoSCoW is a scope-negotiation tool, most appropriate once a set of solutions is already believed valuable (post-discovery) and the live question is what fits inside a delivery constraint, in contrast to RICE, which ranks initiatives against each other for their expected value irrespective of a shared deadline. It is best used downstream of an outcome-oriented roadmap (see [[vision-and-roadmapping]]) so that "Must have" is judged against a specific stated outcome, rather than against undifferentiated stakeholder preference.

**Kano Model**

The Kano Model is a framework, developed by Noriaki Kano in 1984, for classifying product features by the relationship between how much of a feature is delivered and how satisfied customers are as a result. It defines several categories. Basic (or "must-be") features are expected baseline attributes; their presence does not increase satisfaction, but their absence causes strong dissatisfaction (e.g., a car that starts reliably). Performance features have a roughly linear relationship between how much is delivered and how satisfied customers are; more is better and less is worse, in a proportionate way (e.g., fuel efficiency). Excitement (or "delighter") features are unexpected; their absence causes no dissatisfaction because customers did not expect them, but their presence creates disproportionate satisfaction. Indifferent features have no meaningful effect on satisfaction in either direction. A further category, reverse features, satisfy some customer segments while dissatisfying others (some customers want a feature; others are actively bothered by its presence), which is a signal of an unaddressed segmentation problem within the customer base rather than an intrinsic property of the feature.

A key dynamic in the model is that categories are not fixed over time: excitement features migrate toward performance and then toward basic as a market matures and customer expectations rise, because what delights customers when novel becomes the expected baseline once competitors adopt it and customers become accustomed to it. This migration means a company cannot rely indefinitely on last year's delighters to differentiate.

Classification is typically done via the Kano survey method, which asks customers a paired functional/dysfunctional question for each candidate feature ("how would you feel if this feature were present" and "how would you feel if this feature were absent"), with response combinations mapped to a category via a standard scoring table.

Known limitations: the survey method is time-consuming to run properly and requires a reasonably sized, representative sample to produce a stable classification; a small or unrepresentative sample can misclassify a feature. Category migration means a classification has a shelf life and needs to be periodically re-run, particularly in categories with fast-moving competitive dynamics. The model classifies satisfaction impact but does not by itself estimate cost or effort, so it must be paired with an effort-aware method (such as RICE or MoSCoW) to reach a prioritisation decision, not used as a complete prioritisation method on its own.

Connections: Kano provides the "Impact" evidence that RICE otherwise asks a team to estimate from judgment; a feature independently classified as a Kano delighter is a more evidenced high-Impact RICE input than an unvalidated guess. It also gives useful vocabulary for portfolio balance: a roadmap consisting entirely of Basic features will not differentiate the product, and one consisting entirely of Excitement features risks leaving baseline expectations unmet, so a deliberate mix is usually preferable to optimising a single category.
