# Knowledge — Discovery

Covers: Continuous Discovery Habits (Teresa Torres), Jobs to Be Done, Five Whys.

**Continuous Discovery Habits (Teresa Torres)**

Continuous discovery is a practice, articulated by Teresa Torres, in which product teams engage in weekly touchpoints with customers, conducted by the team building the product, tied to specific product decisions. It is positioned as an alternative to periodic, big-batch research conducted by a separate research function and delivered to teams as a report. The core mechanic has three components. First, weekly customer interviews, at least one per week per product trio, small enough in scope to fit continuous cadence rather than a discrete research project. Second, the opportunity solution tree (OST), a visual structure that maps a desired outcome at the root, customer opportunities (needs, pain points, desires surfaced from interviews) as the next layer, candidate solutions beneath each opportunity, and experiments beneath each solution. The tree exists to make the space of possible opportunities and solutions explicit, so teams can compare options rather than committing to the first idea proposed. Third, assumption mapping, in which a team takes a candidate solution and decomposes it into the assumptions that must hold true for it to work, typically sorted along two axes: how important the assumption is and how much evidence currently supports it. Assumptions with high importance and low evidence are prioritized for testing before delivery resources are committed. Torres's interviewing technique emphasizes story-based questions ("tell me about the last time you...") over hypothetical or opinion-based questions ("would you use a feature that..."), on the grounds that recounted specific past behavior is a more reliable data source than speculation about future behavior. Non-leading phrasing is emphasized throughout, since a question that implies the desired answer contaminates the data.

Known limitations: the framework assumes a stable product trio with recurring access to customers or users, which is difficult for teams without direct customer access (e.g., teams several steps removed from end users, or B2B teams selling through intermediaries). Weekly cadence can produce a false sense of rigor if interviews are not planned against a clear evidence goal, becoming a compliance ritual rather than a discovery practice. Small weekly samples are also vulnerable to recency bias and to overweighting vocal or convenient participants unless interview selection is deliberately varied.

Connections: the OST is explicitly the structural tool for operationalizing Cagan's discovery/delivery split and his four risks. Value and usability risk are addressed primarily at the opportunity and solution layers of the tree; feasibility and viability risk surface during assumption mapping when engineering and business assumptions are tested alongside desirability assumptions. Torres's model presumes and reinforces Cagan's empowered team structure, since the trio (PM, designer, engineer) that runs discovery must also own solution choices. It complements Pichler's GO Roadmap (see [[vision-and-roadmapping]]): the roadmap states an outcome and timeframe, and the OST is the mechanism a team uses to discover which opportunities and solutions actually deliver on that stated outcome.

**Jobs to Be Done (Clayton Christensen; refined by Bob Moesta, Tony Ulwick, Alan Klement)**

Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) is a theory of customer behavior holding that customers do not buy products; they "hire" products to make progress against a specific job that arises in a specific circumstance. The theory originates with Clayton Christensen, who popularized it through the "milkshake" example and situated it within his broader work on innovation. It has since been developed by several practitioners with distinct emphases: Bob Moesta developed the Jobs-to-be-Done interview method and the "forces of progress" model through his work on the demand-side sales and switch interview; Tony Ulwick developed Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI), which operationalizes JTBD as a set of measurable, quantifiable desired outcome statements used to prioritize R&D investment; Alan Klement popularized the "job story" format and articulated JTBD as being fundamentally about the customer's desired progress and self-perception, not the product's feature set.

The core mechanic distinguishes a job story from a user story. A user story takes the form "As a [persona], I want [feature], so that [benefit]," which anchors on a persona and a solution. A job story takes the form "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]," which anchors on circumstance and desired progress rather than on a persona or a predetermined feature, on the grounds that circumstance predicts behavior better than persona or demographic segmentation. A job has three dimensions: functional (the practical task to be accomplished), emotional (how the customer wants to feel, or avoid feeling, in the process), and social (how the customer wants to be perceived by others as a result). All three dimensions typically operate simultaneously, and solutions that address only the functional dimension often underperform against ones that also address emotional and social dimensions. The "forces of progress" model (Moesta) explains switching behavior with four forces: push (dissatisfaction with the current situation or product), pull (attraction of the new solution), anxiety (concerns about adopting the new solution), and habit (inertia and allegiance to the existing way of doing things). Adoption occurs when push and pull together outweigh anxiety and habit; the model is used diagnostically to explain why a customer with an unmet need still fails to switch.

In discovery interviews, JTBD is typically used as a lens for interpreting the "why" behind a story elicited through Torres-style story-based questioning: once a customer describes a specific past instance of behavior, the interviewer probes for the circumstance that triggered the need, what alternatives were considered or used, and what progress the customer was ultimately trying to make, including its emotional and social components. The Moesta "switch interview" formalizes this into a timeline reconstruction from first thought of switching through to purchase or adoption.

Known limitations: JTBD interviews are demanding to conduct well, since the technique depends on accurate recall of a specific past episode and skilled probing to surface circumstance and emotional/social dimensions that customers do not volunteer unprompted. The theory is weaker for genuinely novel categories where no prior "hiring" behavior exists to reconstruct. ODI's quantification of outcome statements can create a false sense of precision if the underlying outcome statements were poorly elicited.

Connections: JTBD supplies the substance of the "opportunity" layer in Torres's OST; an opportunity is typically best expressed as a job or an unmet outcome rather than as a feature request. It sharpens Cagan's value risk by giving a concrete unit (progress against a job, including emotional and social dimensions) against which value can be assessed, rather than leaving value undefined. It is also a natural input to Pichler's Product Vision Board (see [[vision-and-roadmapping]]), since the "needs" field on the vision board is most rigorously populated by jobs rather than by feature wishlists.

**Five Whys**

Five Whys is a root cause analysis technique in which a team repeatedly asks "why" (conventionally five times, though the number is heuristic rather than fixed) in response to a problem statement, with each answer forming the basis of the next "why" question, until a root cause is reached that is actionable and, ideally, systemic rather than incidental. The technique originated at Toyota, attributed to Sakichi Toyoda, as part of the Toyota Production System's approach to manufacturing defect analysis. It was popularized in a software and startup product context by Eric Ries in The Lean Startup, who framed it as a tool for proportionate investment: each round of five whys should conclude with a proportionate corrective action, and repeated application of the technique across incidents builds organizational learning about systemic weaknesses.

The core mechanic is a chain of causal questions starting from an observed problem (e.g., a failed release, a churn spike, a support escalation) and drilling downward through successive causes, stopping at a cause that is both sufficiently root (addressing it prevents recurrence, not just this instance) and sufficiently actionable (the team can actually intervene on it). It is typically run as a facilitated group exercise immediately after an incident, while details are fresh, and is documented so the resulting corrective action and reasoning chain are auditable later.

Common failure modes are well documented. Stopping too early is the most frequent failure, where the chain halts at a proximate cause that is easy to state but does not prevent recurrence (e.g., "the deploy script had a bug" rather than the process gap that allowed an untested script to reach production). Blaming people rather than systems is a second failure mode, where a "why" chain terminates at "an engineer made a mistake," which is rarely actionable and discourages honest incident reporting; a well-run chain continues past individual error to ask why the system permitted that error to cause the observed harm. Single-cause bias is a third failure mode: Five Whys models causation as a single linear chain, but many real problems are multi-causal, with several independent or interacting factors contributing to the outcome. Applying a strictly linear technique to a multi-causal problem produces a superficially tidy but incomplete or misleading explanation; some practitioners address this by branching the "why" chain into multiple parallel chains (closer to a fishbone/Ishikawa structure) rather than forcing a single line of inquiry.

Connections: Five Whys is a delivery-side and operational diagnostic tool, distinct in purpose from Torres's and JTBD's customer-facing discovery techniques, but it is the natural technique for interrogating feasibility risk in Cagan's framing, particularly after a delivery failure, outage, or missed estimate, where the question is why the team's engineering process produced the failure rather than why a customer behaves a certain way. It can also be applied, with care, to viability or adoption problems (for example, "why did this cohort not activate") but the multi-causal failure mode is especially acute in that application, since adoption failures are rarely single-cause, and Five Whys should there be treated as a starting hypothesis-generation step feeding into an assumption map rather than as a conclusive diagnosis.
