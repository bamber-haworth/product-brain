# Knowledge — Stakeholder Management

Covers: RACI (and DACI), stakeholder power/interest mapping.

**RACI and DACI**

RACI is a responsibility-assignment matrix used to clarify roles on a cross-functional initiative or decision, mapping each person or function involved against one of four roles for each task or decision: Responsible (does the work), Accountable (owns the outcome and has final sign-off; convention holds there should be exactly one Accountable per task or decision, to avoid diffused ownership), Consulted (input is sought before a decision, two-way communication), and Informed (told after a decision or at defined checkpoints, one-way communication). It is typically built as a grid with tasks or decisions down one axis and people or roles across the other, with each cell populated by one or more of the four letters.

DACI is a related variant, more commonly used specifically for decision-making rather than general task responsibility, with roles Driver (runs the process of reaching the decision, keeps it moving, is not necessarily the decision-maker), Approver (makes the final call; as with RACI's Accountable, convention holds there should be exactly one), Contributors (provide input, analogous to RACI's Consulted), and Informed (told the outcome, analogous to RACI's Informed). The distinction some practitioners draw between RACI and DACI is that RACI is oriented toward ongoing operational task ownership, while DACI is oriented toward a single, bounded decision with a clear before/after point.

The core discipline in both models is forcing exactly one name into the "final say" role (Accountable in RACI, Approver in DACI) per task or decision. The most common failure this discipline is designed to prevent is a decision or task with no single owner, where diffused accountability across a committee causes the item to stall, or with multiple people believing they hold final say, causing conflicting direction to be given to the team doing the work.

Known limitations: a RACI or DACI matrix is a coordination artifact, not a substitute for actually resolving a disagreement about what should be decided; it clarifies who decides, not what the right decision is. Matrices can become stale quickly on a fast-moving initiative and, if not actively maintained, create false confidence that roles are still accurate. Overuse (building a formal matrix for every minor task) creates process overhead disproportionate to the coordination problem actually being solved; the technique is best reserved for genuinely cross-functional, ambiguous-ownership situations rather than applied uniformly.

Connections: RACI/DACI is the mechanism that operationalises Cagan's insistence that product teams, not committees, own solution decisions; naming a single Accountable/Approver on product decisions is a concrete way to prevent an empowered team from being quietly overridden by a diffuse stakeholder group. It is commonly paired with stakeholder power/interest mapping (below): the mapping identifies who matters and how, and RACI/DACI then assigns each identified stakeholder a specific role on specific decisions.

**Stakeholder power/interest mapping**

Stakeholder mapping is a technique for segmenting the people and groups affected by or able to affect an initiative according to two dimensions: their power (ability to influence the initiative's direction or resourcing) and their interest (degree to which they care about or are affected by the outcome). Plotting stakeholders on a two-by-two grid of power and interest is commonly attributed to Aubrey Mendelow (1991) and is widely used in both general management and product/programme contexts.

The grid produces four quadrants, each implying a different engagement strategy. High power, high interest stakeholders should be managed closely, with direct, frequent engagement, since they can materially affect the initiative and are motivated to do so. High power, low interest stakeholders should be kept satisfied, meaning engaged enough that they do not become blockers, but without consuming disproportionate time given their lower current interest, since low interest can shift quickly if the initiative starts to affect them. Low power, high interest stakeholders should be kept informed, since their engagement and goodwill matter but they cannot independently redirect the initiative. Low power, low interest stakeholders require only minimal, low-effort monitoring.

The core discipline is deliberately calibrating communication effort to each quadrant rather than communicating uniformly to everyone with equal frequency and depth, since uniform communication over-serves low-power/low-interest stakeholders at the expense of under-serving high-power/high-interest ones, or produces update fatigue across the board.

Known limitations: power and interest are not static and should be reassessed as an initiative progresses, since a stakeholder's position on the grid can shift materially (a low-interest executive can become high-interest the moment budget or headcount is affected). The technique also does not resolve what to do when a high-power, high-interest stakeholder is opposed to the initiative's direction; it identifies who needs managing closely but not how to resolve substantive disagreement with them. Mapping is also vulnerable to being done from a single perspective without checking it against other team members' read of the same stakeholders, which can miss power that is informal or indirect (e.g., influence through a peer network rather than formal authority).

Connections: stakeholder mapping typically precedes and informs a RACI/DACI matrix; the map identifies who the relevant stakeholders are and how much engagement each warrants, and RACI/DACI then assigns each stakeholder a specific formal role on specific decisions. It is also directly relevant to Cagan's empowered-team framing as a diagnostic tool: a product team that finds itself needing to "manage closely" an unusually large number of high-power stakeholders on routine product decisions is a signal of an underlying organisational structure closer to a feature factory than an empowered team.
