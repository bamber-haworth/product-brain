# Knowledge — Goal Setting

Covers: OKRs, North Star Metric.

**OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)**

OKRs are a goal-setting framework consisting of an Objective (a qualitative, ambitious, memorable statement of what is to be achieved) paired with a small set of Key Results (quantitative, measurable outcomes that indicate whether the objective has been achieved). The framework originates with Andy Grove at Intel and was later popularised in Silicon Valley by John Doerr, who applied it at Google. Roman Pichler has adapted OKRs specifically for product teams, emphasising that Key Results should be stated as customer and business outcomes (behaviour change, adoption, revenue, retention) rather than as delivery milestones (features shipped, projects completed), on the grounds that a shipped feature is not evidence of achieved value.

The core mechanic separates the Objective, which should be qualitative and inspiring enough to align a team without needing a number, from the Key Results, which must be objectively measurable so that achievement is not a matter of opinion at the end of the period. A well-formed Key Result answers "how would we know we got there," not "what did we build." Typically an Objective has two to five Key Results; more than that dilutes focus. OKRs are usually set on a quarterly or similar fixed cadence, reviewed partway through, and scored at the end, with scoring conventions varying by organisation (Google's convention scores on a 0.0–1.0 scale, treating 0.6–0.7 as a good outcome for an intentionally ambitious objective, on the premise that hitting 1.0 consistently means the objective was set too low). A frequently cited discipline, particularly in Doerr's account, is decoupling OKR scoring from individual performance review, since a fully honest, ambitious OKR is likely to be missed on the strict 1.0 scale, and tying missed OKRs to compensation or ratings incentivises sandbagging.

Known limitations: OKRs are frequently implemented as disguised feature roadmaps, with Key Results written as delivery milestones ("ship X by Q3") rather than outcomes, which defeats the framework's purpose and is the most common failure mode observed in practice. Cascading OKRs rigidly top-down (company OKR to team OKR to individual OKR, mechanically decomposed) can produce local optimisation and busywork disconnected from actual strategic judgment; Google's own practice treats OKRs as more loosely aligned than strictly cascaded. Quarterly cadence can mismatch initiatives whose feedback loops are longer or shorter than a quarter, producing either premature judgment or drift.

Connections: Pichler positions outcome-oriented Key Results as the natural complement to the GO (Goal-Oriented) Product Roadmap (see [[vision-and-roadmapping]]): the roadmap states desired outcomes with a timeframe at a coarser grain, and OKRs operationalise the current quarter's slice of that roadmap into measurable results. Cagan's outcome-over-output framing is essentially the same discipline stated differently; a Key Result written as an output is the same failure mode Cagan describes as the "feature factory."

**North Star Metric**

The North Star Metric (NSM) is a single metric chosen to represent the core value a product delivers to customers, selected so that its movement is believed to causally predict long-term business success (typically revenue or sustainable growth). The concept is associated with Sean Ellis and was substantially popularised through Amplitude's product and marketing work, notably by Amplitude's Growth team, as a tool for aligning cross-functional teams around a single measure of value delivered rather than a scattered set of departmental metrics.

The core mechanic is selecting one metric, not a dashboard, that satisfies several criteria simultaneously: it reflects customer value received (not just company activity, such as signups, which measure acquisition rather than value), it correlates with revenue or business outcomes over time, it is a leading indicator (moves before revenue moves, giving teams an earlier signal than lagging financial metrics), and it is something the whole company, not just one team, can influence and rally around. Commonly cited examples include Facebook's early "monthly active users who added 7 friends in 10 days" framing (a proxy for reaching a value threshold quickly) and Airbnb's "nights booked." A North Star Metric is typically supported by an "input tree" or set of driver metrics that decompose it into the levers different teams can actually pull (analogous in structure to Torres's opportunity solution tree, though built from metrics rather than customer opportunities).

Known limitations: a single metric is a compression of a complex business and can be gamed or optimised in ways that harm the business if treated as the sole target (Goodhart's law risk); it should be paired with guardrail metrics that catch value-destroying optimisation (e.g., an engagement NSM paired with a quality or satisfaction guardrail to prevent engagement being juiced by dark patterns). Selecting a North Star Metric is genuinely difficult for multi-sided marketplaces or multi-product portfolios, where a single number may not represent value well across distinct customer segments or product lines, and some practitioners in that situation use one North Star Metric per major segment or line rather than forcing a single company-wide number.

Connections: the North Star Metric sits above OKRs in a typical goal hierarchy; the NSM answers "what does our product need to deliver to succeed long-term," and OKRs for a given period answer "what will we do this quarter to move it or one of its drivers." It also gives a natural evaluation criterion for Cagan's value risk: an initiative that does not plausibly move the North Star Metric or one of its named drivers is a candidate for deprioritisation regardless of its RICE or MoSCoW score (see [[prioritisation]]).
