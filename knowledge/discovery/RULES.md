# Rules — Discovery

Confirmed patterns to apply by default. Demote to `HYPOTHESES.md` if later contradicted.

1. Run customer interviews weekly, in small batches, tied to a live product decision, rather than as periodic large-batch research projects. Continuous cadence surfaces disconfirming evidence before a solution has consumed delivery resources; big-batch research surfaces it only after.

2. Structure interview questions around a specific past instance of behaviour ("tell me about the last time you...") rather than hypothetical or opinion-based questions ("would you use..."). Recounted past behaviour is more reliable evidence of what a customer will actually do than stated future intent.

3. Before writing a feature request or user story, restate the underlying need as a job story ("when [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]"). This anchors the requirement on circumstance and desired progress rather than on a persona or a predetermined solution, and keeps the solution space open longer.

4. When assessing a job or opportunity, check all three dimensions: functional, emotional, and social. A solution that satisfies only the functional dimension is incomplete evidence of value; ask what the customer wants to feel and how they want to be perceived, not only what task they are trying to complete.

5. Before committing delivery resources to a solution, map its assumptions on two axes (importance, existing evidence) and test the assumptions that are both high-importance and low-evidence first. Do not let assumption testing default to whichever assumption is easiest to test.

6. When running root cause analysis (Five Whys) on a single-cause problem such as a delivery failure or outage, continue past the first proximate or individual-blame answer to a systemic cause that is both root and actionable. Stopping at "an engineer made a mistake" is a signal the chain was cut short, not a valid root cause.

7. Do not apply Five Whys' single linear chain to a problem that is plausibly multi-causal (e.g., an adoption or retention problem). Branch into parallel causal chains, or treat Five Whys output as a hypothesis list to be tested via assumption mapping rather than as a conclusive diagnosis.
