# Knowledge — Vision and Roadmapping

Covers: Product Vision Board, GO (Goal-Oriented) Product Roadmap (Roman Pichler).

**Product Vision Board (Roman Pichler)**

The Product Vision Board is a one-page strategic tool developed by Roman Pichler for articulating and aligning stakeholders around a product's vision before roadmap or feature work begins. It structures the vision into a small set of fields typically comprising: the vision itself (a concise statement of the ultimate purpose and desired future state the product exists to create), the target group (the market segment(s) the product serves, ideally defined in needs-based terms, see [[gtm-strategy]]), the needs (the customer problems or jobs the product addresses, ideally expressed as jobs rather than feature wishlists, see [[discovery]]), the product (a short description of the product concept, kept deliberately brief so the board does not collapse into a feature list), and the business goals (what the company gains from the product succeeding: revenue, market position, strategic value).

The core discipline of the board is ordering and constraint: vision and target group and needs are meant to be established before the product description is filled in, so that the offering is derived from a stated need rather than the need being retrofitted to justify an already-decided feature set. The board is intentionally kept to one page and revisited periodically (Pichler recommends treating it as a living document, reviewed and updated as evidence accumulates), rather than authored once and archived.

Known limitations: because the board is deliberately terse, it can be completed superficially, with fields filled in as plausible-sounding statements rather than as evidenced claims; the board's quality is entirely dependent on the rigor of the discovery work behind each field, particularly needs and target group. It is a strategic alignment tool, not a validation tool, and does not by itself provide evidence that the stated needs are real or that the target group will respond to the product as conceived; that validation is the job of continuous discovery (see [[discovery]]).

Connections: the Vision Board is the natural upstream input to the GO Product Roadmap (below); the roadmap's goals should trace back to the business goals and needs stated on the vision board, and a roadmap item with no visible link back to the vision board is a signal of drift. It also functions as a viability and value-risk checkpoint in Cagan's framing, since the business goals field forces an explicit statement of why the company should care, alongside the customer-facing value case.

**GO (Goal-Oriented) Product Roadmap (Roman Pichler)**

The GO Product Roadmap is a roadmap format developed by Roman Pichler as an explicit alternative to a feature-based roadmap. Rather than listing features and target ship dates, a GO Roadmap states a small number of goals for each time horizon (Pichler's format typically uses named, coarse horizons such as "now," "next," and "later," rather than fixed calendar quarters, to avoid over-committing to precise dates for distant, less certain work) and leaves the specific features or solutions that will achieve each goal deliberately open, to be determined through ongoing discovery rather than fixed at roadmap authoring time.

The core mechanic is that each roadmap entry is phrased as an outcome to be achieved (e.g., "reduce onboarding drop-off" or "establish a foothold in [segment]") together with a rough timeframe and, if useful, a metric that will indicate success, rather than as a named feature and a date. This is the same discipline OKRs apply to Key Results (see [[goal-setting]]), applied at the coarser grain of a multi-month or multi-quarter roadmap rather than a single quarter's Key Results. Committing to a goal rather than a specific solution preserves the team's ability to change solution approach as discovery surfaces better options, without that change reading as a broken roadmap promise, since the promise was the outcome, not the specific feature.

Known limitations: a goal-oriented roadmap is harder to communicate to stakeholders (particularly sales, marketing, or executive audiences) who are accustomed to and often explicitly want specific feature commitments with fixed dates, especially in contexts involving external commitments (contractual delivery dates, marketing launches tied to a specific capability). Reconciling a GO Roadmap with these commitment-driven audiences typically requires a separate, more concrete release plan derived from the roadmap once solutions are chosen, rather than exposing the goal-oriented roadmap directly as a delivery commitment. A goal-oriented roadmap can also be used as cover for a lack of planning discipline if goals are stated too vaguely to ever be judged as met or missed; a goal without an associated success metric is a weaker version of the format.

Connections: the GO Roadmap sits directly between the Product Vision Board (above, states the overall vision and business goals) and OKRs (see [[goal-setting]], states the current quarter's measurable results); it is the connective layer that translates a multi-quarter vision into a sequence of goal-oriented horizons, one slice of which becomes the current period's OKRs. It also depends on continuous discovery (see [[discovery]]) to populate the open solution space beneath each stated goal; a GO Roadmap without an active discovery practice behind it has stated outcomes with no mechanism for finding out how to achieve them.
